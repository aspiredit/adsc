Why we built this A 2022 night at a school event. Three dads. One conversation that wouldn't end.

The three of us met at one of those school functions where parents drift toward the snack table because there's nothing else to do. We started talking. Then we kept talking. Then we walked outside to keep talking, because none of us had said any of this out loud in years.

Our wives were in support groups. Our kids were in therapy. Our mothers-in-law had book clubs. We had nothing. We had nobody. We had a quiet contract to be steady, to be the rock, to keep our hands on the wheel.

So we built it. A room for the dads in the background. A pool hall on a Saturday night. A barbecue with the kids running wild and nobody apologizing. A text thread that buzzes when someone's had a hard week. The thing we should have had in 2018 when the diagnosis came. The thing we're going to keep building, one meetup at a time, for every dad who needs it next.

We can't fix the diagnosis. We can't make the IEPs easier or the meltdowns shorter. What we can do is make sure you don't carry it alone.

— Jonathan, Jesse & Emmanuel

Common questions

Things dads actually ask us.

Is this only for dads in Houston?
In-person meetups and family events are Houston-area (we run them everywhere from Pearland to The Woodlands to Sugar Land to Bellaire). The newsletter, podcast, member text thread, and Survival Guide are open to dads anywhere. We have Charter members in Austin, Dallas, San Antonio, and a few out-of-state dads who joined to help fund the work.
What if my child is grown? Or recently diagnosed?
Both. Our youngest member's son was diagnosed three months ago. Our oldest member's daughter is twenty-six. The point isn't the age of the kid. It's the experience of being the dad. Every stage has its own particular weight.
Is this a support group? A therapy thing?
No. Nobody runs a session. Nobody passes around a feelings stick. It's a social club. We meet at pool halls, sports bars, barbecues, bowling alleys. Things happen that look like support sometimes, because that's what happens when men actually talk. But the format is just hanging out.
Do I need to be religious? Any political affiliation?
No on both counts. We are deliberately nondenominational and nonpolitical. We have brothers across every faith and no faith, every political stripe, every kind of household. The diagnosis doesn't care about any of that and neither do we.
What does a meetup actually look like?
Show up at the announced location around 6:30. Find the table with our reserved sign. Order a drink and food. Introduce yourself. Talk about whatever. Some nights it's three hours of football and ribbing. Some nights someone shares something heavy and the table goes quiet for ten seconds and then someone says the right thing. You can leave whenever you want.
Why pay for membership if events are free?
Honest answer: because nobody's paying us to do this. We rent the spaces, we buy the supplies for family events, we pay for the website and the podcast hosting. Membership funds the next meetup. It also funds scholarships for dads who couldn't otherwise afford to come (yes, some dads can't afford the Uber fare to a free pool night). And it's tax-deductible.
How do I bring my family to a family event?
RSVP through the events page when one's announced (usually 4-6 weeks out). Tell us how many adults and how many kids and the kids' ages. There's no charge. We just need a head count for food and space. Spouses, partners, neurotypical siblings, grandparents — everyone's welcome.
Can I just come once and see how it feels?
Yes. That's how almost everyone starts. Come to one meetup. Buy your own beer. See what it's like. If it's not for you, no hard feelings, you're still on the newsletter. If it is for you, we'll see you next month.
